The quiet repricing: why midcap earnings are rewriting the playbook

For two years the story was megacap dominance. A new quarter of filings suggests a different kind of discipline is winning — lean operators with pricing power are outpacing their larger rivals.

Sarah Mendel
Markets editor · London
21 Apr 2026 · 07:10 · Updated 09:14 BST
In this story: FTSE:MCX NYSE:SPY Rightmove Games Workshop Ashley Steel Consumer

The quarter began with the assumption that midcaps were a trade, not a story. By the time the filings closed, both halves of that sentence looked wrong. Across the FTSE:MCX and the US equivalents, a group of companies largely ignored by the megacap narrative reported margins their larger rivals couldn't match — and, more interestingly, forward guidance their larger rivals wouldn't commit to.

What changed isn't demand. Demand has been soft, unevenly, across most of the cycle. What changed is that a cohort of operators — mostly in consumer, industrial distribution, and specialty software — finished the capital-discipline work they began in 2023. Headcount is leaner. Leases are shorter. Pricing is sharper. And the customer base is stickier than it was.

"We stopped chasing revenue that required us to apologise for our prices," one FTSE 250 CFO told Business Fortitude, asking for anonymity because the company is in a quiet period.

"We stopped chasing revenue that required us to apologise for our prices. The customers who were annoyed by the price were also the ones costing us the most to serve." FTSE 250 CFO · On background

What the filings actually show

Strip out the top decile of the index and the picture is different from the headline one. In Q1 2026 filings through last Friday, the median FTSE 250 operator expanded gross margin by 140 basis points year over year. The median megacap expanded by 30.

That's a wider gap than any quarter since the period right before the GFC. It is not, on its own, a signal to rotate. It is a signal that the assumptions baked into index construction are starting to look stale. Rightmove's most recent update, with its quiet 11% rise in ARPU, is a better example than any macro chart.

Key takeaways

  • Median FTSE 250 gross margin expanded 140bps YoY in Q1 2026; megacap peers expanded 30bps.
  • Pricing power — not revenue growth — is the differentiator. Operators who held price in 2023–24 are now compounding on the base.
  • The repricing is most visible in consumer specialty and industrial distribution; least visible in large-cap tech.
  • Capital-allocation discipline (buybacks over M&A, shorter leases, tighter headcount) is correlated with the outperformance.

The operators who got there first

If you want to see what pricing discipline looks like in practice, Games Workshop is still the most-studied case — and the most misunderstood. The usual reading is that a niche business can charge what it likes; the more useful reading is that it spent a decade teaching its customer base what the product was actually worth.

Three patterns that show up in the data

First, buybacks have taken the place of M&A. Median announced buyback authorisation across the index is up 28% year over year; announced acquisition value is down 19%.

Second, guidance tenor has changed. More midcaps are guiding to full-year operating margin than at any point in the last five quarters; fewer are guiding to revenue.

Third, capex is discretionary again. It's being directed toward efficiency rather than capacity — Ashley Steel's comments to shareholders last month were the cleanest articulation of this shift.

The board's job in this cycle is to make sure the next round of capex is earning its seat. We're not funding anything that can't show us a clean 12-month payback. That's not austerity. That's discipline.

Why the narrative lags

The repricing has been visible to operators for two quarters. It has been visible to analysts for one. It is only now becoming visible to the broader market — and the broader market is where the narrative gets written. Expect the framing to shift through the summer.

For readers who care less about the index and more about what this implies for their own business: pricing power is the thing to protect. The window in which you can hold a price increase without losing material volume is longer than most operators believe.

None of this is a forecast about where the cycle goes next. It is a claim about where the discipline has settled. The midcap re-rating is not a trade. It is the visible outcome of two years of quieter work.
